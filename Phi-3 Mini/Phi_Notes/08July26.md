### methods note: measuring the memorisation directly (Ahmed et al. 2026)

right now the "Phi memorises but does not generalise" claim rests on the train-val gap (train falls, val stays at random baseline, so it must be fitting the seen blocks). Ahmed, Cooper, Koyejo, Liang, "Extracting books from production language models" (arXiv:2601.02671v1, Stanford and Yale) 
is a paper that measures memorisation with `nv-recall`, a block-based longest-common-substring score between a model's continuation and the source text. 

Using Ahmed et al approach, helpful for testing Phi's outputs are the concept (memorisation vs generalisation) and the metric (`nv-recall` / LCS verbatim overlap).

the test for the 2x2, decided at generation: aim is to prompt Phi P2 and Mistral P2 with a Wake passage prefix, generate the continuation, and measure verbatim overlap against the actual training text.

- **prediction:** Phi (memorised) regurgitates the training block near-verbatim, high `nv-recall`. Mistral (deepest reorganisation in the lineup, generalised) produces novel Wake-style forms not in the training text, low `nv-recall` with high novelty.
- if it holds, the finding is something along the lines of "textbook **memorises** where internet **transforms**."

the duckweed repo's n-gram overlap and gzip compression-novelty are the same family as `nv-recall` (verbatim-reuse measures).
